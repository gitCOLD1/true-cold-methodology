
# TRUE COLD — True Cost of Living Data

### Public Domain Methodology | CC0 1.0 | No Rights Reserved

TRUE COLD is an alternative cost-of-living index built entirely from 
primary-source transaction data, independent of Consumer Price Index 
(CPI) methodology. It covers 1987–2025 and is updated annually.

**SSRN Working Paper:** https://papers.ssrn.com/sol3/papers.cfm?abstract_id=6489599

**License:** CC0 1.0 Universal — Public Domain Dedication. No rights 
reserved. Use freely, no attribution required.

## The Index in One Number

Since 1987, TRUE COLD shows cumulative cost-of-living increases of 
approximately 330%. The CPI reports 181% over the same period. The 
149 percentage-point gap is the quantitative expression of four decades 
of systematic understatement.

Since 2011 (first year of comparable Truflation data):
- TRUE COLD: +85%
- Truflation: +46%
- CPI: +46%

## Key Findings

- A household needing $50,000 in 1987 to maintain a middle-class 
  standard of living would need approximately $215,000 in 2025 under 
  TRUE COLD — not the $140,500 that CPI implies.
- The $74,500 gap is not wealth foregone. It is the invisible erosion 
  of purchasing power accumulated across nearly four decades of 
  understated inflation.
- The three primary drivers of the gap: Owner's Equivalent Rent 
  (housing), provider-receipt-based healthcare measurement, and a 
  goods basket anchored to import rather than retail prices.

## Methodology Notes

**Base year:** 1987 = 100. Annual chain-linked from December readings.

**Housing splice:** Case-Shiller national (CSUSHPINSA) is the primary 
series 1987–2006. From 2007 forward, TRUE COLD uses a 50/50 blend of 
Case-Shiller and Zillow ZHVI. Both use actual closed transaction 
records. At the splice point they are nearly identical.

**Healthcare:** KFF Employer Health Benefits Survey tracks total 
employer-sponsored family premiums annually since 1987, through HIAA 
(1987–91), KPMG (1991–98), Kaiser/HRET (1999–2017), and KFF 
(2018–present). Total premium is used regardless of employer/employee 
split — it is real economic cost either way.

**Weights:** Set to approximate CPI basket proportions with two upward 
adjustments: education/childcare (4% vs CPI's ~2%) and healthcare 
(9% vs CPI's 8.3%), reflecting actual spending shares for working 
families.
